Nonprofits and Immigrant Integration

Immigrants face barriers accessing government services due to language, administrative complexity, and limited legal rights.

📋

Service Providers

Delivering legal, social, and employment services that immigrants cannot access through public institutions

🤝

Mediating Organizations

Bridging immigrants and mainstream society through civic incorporation and representation

📡

Disseminators of Values

Transmitting new perspectives to local communities, reducing prejudice and enhancing understanding

Key Insight: The disseminative function of NPOs — spreading new values and knowledge — has been underexplored in the literature.

Research Gap & Purpose

What We Know

  • Individual and social factors shaping attitudes toward immigrants
  • NPOs as service providers and mediators for immigrants
  • Research concentrated in Europe & North America

What We Don't Know

  • Whether NPOs directly influence public acceptance of immigrants
  • How organizational characteristics shape this influence
  • Whether findings apply to non-Western, emerging immigration contexts
Research Question: Do nonprofit organizational characteristics — specifically member heterogeneity vs. homogeneity — differentially affect local residents' acceptance of immigrants?
Part II

Theoretical Framework

Organizational Characteristics & Acceptance

Theoretical Foundations

Disseminative Function (Frumkin, 2002)

NPOs provide new values, perspectives, and knowledge to communities — correcting misconceptions and fostering acceptance through information sharing.

Social Capital Theory (Putnam, 2000)

Bridging social capital (heterogeneous ties) promotes openness to diversity, while bonding social capital (homogeneous ties) reinforces ingroup solidarity.

Mediating Structures (Berger & Neuhaus, 1977)

NPOs mediate between individuals and larger institutions, shaping how communities encounter and process diversity.

Organizational Characteristics (Anheier, 2006)

NPOs are not monolithic — their mission, composition, and constituency vary, shaping the nature and impact of their activities.

Heterogeneous vs. Homogeneous NPOs

Heterogeneous NPOs

Membership: Diverse backgrounds
Examples: Voluntary orgs, environmental groups, advocacy orgs, hobby groups, educational associations

Mechanism: Exposure to diverse perspectives fosters multidimensional thinking, cross-cultural understanding, and acceptance of immigrants

Homogeneous NPOs

Membership: Shared backgrounds
Examples: Occupational associations, alumni groups, hometown associations, kinship groups

Mechanism: Reinforces existing perspectives, limits exchange of diverse viewpoints, may reduce openness to immigrants

Hypotheses

H1

Nonprofit organizations engaged in immigrant-related activities are positively associated with public acceptance of immigrants.

H2a

Participation in heterogeneous nonprofit organizations is positively associated with public acceptance of immigrants.

H2b

Participation in homogeneous nonprofit organizations is negatively associated with public acceptance of immigrants.

Discussion: Why the Unexpected H1 Result?

Counterintuitive Finding: More NPOs in a neighborhood = lower immigrant acceptance (β = −0.351***)

Possible Explanations

  • Selection effect: NPOs concentrate in communities with high immigrant density — where tensions are already salient
  • Residents perceive immigrant-related issues as pressing local problems requiring resolution
  • Low-density or superficial contact may heighten threat perceptions rather than foster acceptance (Bentsen, 2022)

Implications

  • NPO presence reflects community salience of immigrant tensions, not a direct acceptance mechanism
  • Mere organizational presence is insufficient — the quality of engagement matters
  • NPOs should cultivate dense and meaningful relationships with local residents

Discussion: Organizational Characteristics Matter

H2a Supported (β = +0.552, p < 0.01)
  • Diverse membership exposes individuals to varied perspectives and cross-cultural knowledge
  • Fosters multidimensional thinking about social phenomena
  • Deepens understanding of culturally distinct practices
  • Consistent with Putnam's bridging social capital
H2b Direction Consistent (β = −0.175, p = .173)
  • Shared backgrounds lead to uniform information circulation
  • Reinforces existing perspectives rather than facilitating diverse exchange
  • Consistent with bonding social capital mechanisms
  • With refined measurement, the effect may reach statistical significance
Key Takeaway: The divergence between heterogeneous (+) and homogeneous (−) NPOs confirms that organizational composition matters in shaping attitudes toward immigrants.

Part V

Contributions & Implications

What This Means for Research & Practice

Scholarly Contributions

1. Theoretical Advancement

Explicates the disseminative role of NPOs as a determinant of immigrant acceptance, moving beyond service provision and mediation frameworks to focus on value transmission mechanisms.

2. Organizational Characteristics as Differentiator

Demonstrates that NPOs are not monolithic: heterogeneous and homogeneous organizations exert divergent effects on acceptance — a critical nuance absent from prior research.

3. Non-Western Context

Extends immigration research to South Korea, enriching the literature with insights from a culturally distinct, rapidly diversifying society.

Practical & Policy Implications

✅

Support Heterogeneous NPOs

Policies should promote diverse civic associations that bring together members from varied backgrounds — these are the organizations that measurably increase immigrant acceptance.

💬

Deepen Community Engagement

NPO presence alone is insufficient. Organizations must cultivate dense, meaningful interactions with local residents to transform attitudes, not just signal immigrant-related tensions.

For Policymakers: Rather than simply increasing NPO numbers in immigrant-dense areas, invest in programs that promote cross-cultural dialogue through diverse civic participation.

Limitations & Future Research

Limitations

  • Single city case: Suwon may not represent broader Korean or non-Western contexts
  • Cross-sectional data: Limits causal inference; panel data would strengthen findings
  • Narrow NPO measurement: Only counts organizations; does not capture quality, scope, or intensity of activities
  • Self-reported data: Possible measurement imprecision in perception-based items

Future Directions

  • Use nationally representative data across multiple Korean cities
  • Employ longitudinal designs to establish causal pathways
  • Develop multi-dimensional measures of NPO activity (types, intensity, quality of engagement)
  • Explore cross-national comparisons with other emerging immigration destinations in Asia
